# Shahi Mutton Nihari
The ultimate slow-cooked Mughlai breakfast elite, Shahi Mutton Nihari is a deep, velvety, flour-thickened marrow stew cooked overnight with shank cuts of meat and a complex bouquet of therapeutic spices.

## Place of Origin
Old Delhi, India.

## Backstory
Nihari originates from the Arabic word *Nahar*, which translates to "morning." It was developed in the late 18th century in Old Delhi as a medicinal breakfast for the working-class laborers and royal soldiers. Cooked overnight in massive copper cauldrons (*Deghs*), it was consumed right after sunrise prayers to provide maximum energy and stamina for heavy physical labor during harsh winters. It quickly climbed the social ladder to reach the royal tables of the Mughal court due to its incredible depth of flavor.

## Ingredients
### Main Components
* **Mutton:** 1kg Mutton Shank cuts (Nalli and Bong pieces are essential)
* **Fat:** 1/2 cup Asli Ghee or Mustard Oil

### The Nihari Potli Spice Mix (Ground Coarsely)
* **Saunf (Fennel Seeds):** 2 tbsp
* **Sonth (Dry Ginger Powder):** 1 tbsp
* **Sabut Dhania (Coriander Seeds):** 1 tbsp
* **Jeera (Cumin Seeds):** 1 tsp
* **Pipli (Long Pepper):** 3 pieces (Crucial for authentic Nihari flavor)
* **Jaiphal & Javitri (Nutmeg/Mace):** 1/4 tsp each
* **Badi Elaichi:** 2 pods

### Base & Thickener
* **Pyaz (Onion):** 2 medium onions, finely sliced
* **Adrak-Lahsun Paste:** 2 tbsp
* **Atta (Whole Wheat Flour):** 1/4 cup (Roasted lightly on a dry pan)
* **Kashmiri Lal Mirch Powder:** 2 tsp (for deep red color)

### Garnish (Essential Finisher)
* **Adrak:** 2-inch piece, sliced into paper-thin juliennes
* **Hari Mirch:** 3, finely chopped
* **Hara Dhania:** 1/4 cup, chopped
* **Nimbu:** 2 lemons, cut into wedges

## Preparation
1. **Spice Blend:** Grind all the components listed under the Nihari Potli Spice Mix into a relatively smooth powder. This is your fresh homemade Nihari Masala.
2. **Flour Slurry:** Dissolve the lightly roasted whole wheat flour (*atta*) into 1 cup of room-temperature water, mixing thoroughly with a fork to ensure zero lumps are present.

## Cooking Steps
1. **Searing the Shank:** Heat asli ghee in a heavy, deep-bottomed pot. Fry the sliced onions until soft and light golden. Add the mutton shank cuts (Bong and Nalli) along with the adrak-lahsun paste. Cook on high heat for 5-7 minutes, sealing the meat juices until the exterior surface looks opaque.
2. **Spice Infusion:** Add 3 tablespoons of your freshly made Nihari Masala, Kashmiri red chilli powder, and salt. Sauté for 2 minutes. Add 4 to 5 cups of water, bringing the liquid to a rolling boil.
3. **The Royal Slow Cook:** Lower the heat to the absolute minimum setting. Cover the pot with a tight lid, weighting it down with a heavy object (traditional chefs seal the edges with raw wheat dough to trap steam). Let it slow-cook continuously for 5 to 6 hours until the meat is incredibly tender and literally falling off the bone structure.
4. **Thickening the Stew:** Open the lid. Slowly pour the wheat flour slurry into the simmering stew while stirring non-stop to prevent lumps. Simmer on low heat for another 15 minutes. The gravy will immediately transform into a thick, glossy, velvety smooth broth.
5. **The Final Assembly:** Turn off the heat. Let the dish stand for 10 minutes. The glorious red oil (*Tari*) will float up to the surface. Scoop this oil out carefully into a separate bowl to serve on top of individual portions later.

## When to Eat
* **Occasions:** Traditionally served exclusively as an early morning weekend breakfast treat during colder winter months.
* **Meal Timing:** Best consumed between 6:00 AM and 9:30 AM for a highly authentic dining experience.

## With What to Eat
* **Accompaniments:** Must be paired hot with thick, leavened *Khameiri Roti* fresh out of a clay tandoor.
* **Condiments:** Individual bowls must be customized at the table by piling on fresh ginger juliennes, chopped green chillies, fresh coriander leaves, and a heavy squeeze of lemon juice.

## Chef's Secrets
* **The Tari Protection:** The addition of wheat flour slurry (*atta*) absorbs all the fat and oil from the gravy, making the dish look dull. Always skim off the red grease layer (*Tari*) right before adding the slurry and store it safely. Pour it back over the individual bowls when serving to give it that rich restaurant appearance.
* **The Pipli Secret:** Authentic Nihari gets its signature warm, lingering, throat-clearing heat not from standard black pepper or red chillies, but from *Pipli* (Long Pepper). Skipping Pipli transforms the complex flavor of a genuine imperial Delhi Nihari into a generic spicy mutton curry.

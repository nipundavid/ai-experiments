# Shahi Mutton Shami Kebab
An authentic Awadhi and Mughlai delicacy, Shami Kebabs are silky, melt-in-the-mouth patties made from finely minced mutton and chana dal, infused with an array of aromatic whole spices.

## Place of Origin
Lucknow, Uttar Pradesh, India (Formerly the Awadh Region).

## Backstory
Legend traces the origin of the Shami Kebab to the royal kitchens of Lucknow for Nawab Asaf-ud-Daula. The Nawab was a passionate gourmet but had lost his teeth due to age and indulgence. He ordered his Khansamas (royal chefs) to create a meat dish so incredibly tender that it required absolutely no chewing to consume. The chefs responded by boiling lean meat with lentils and grinding it into a fine, silky paste before shallow frying, giving birth to this culinary marvel.

## Ingredients
### Main Components
* **Mutton:** 500g lean minced mutton (boneless keema, preferably from the leg)
* **Lentils:** 1/2 cup Chana Dal (Bengal gram), soaked for 2 hours

### Whole Spices (Boiling Mix)
* **Badi Elaichi (Black Cardamom):** 2 pods
* **Choti Elaichi (Green Cardamom):** 4 pods
* **Laung (Cloves):** 5 pieces
* **Dalchini (Cinnamon Stick):** 1-inch piece
* **Sabut Kali Mirch (Black Peppercorns):** 10 pieces
* **Jeera (Cumin Seeds):** 1 tsp
* **Sabut Lal Mirch (Whole Dried Red Chillies):** 4 pieces

### Aromatics & Bind
* **Adrak (Ginger):** 1-inch piece, chopped
* **Lahsun (Garlic):** 8-10 cloves
* **Egg:** 1 whole egg (whisked, for binding)
* **Asli Ghee:** 3-4 tbsp (for shallow frying)

### Fresh Herbs & Stuffing
* **Pyaz (Onion):** 1 medium, very finely chopped
* **Hari Mirch (Green Chillies):** 2-3, finely chopped
* **Pudina (Fresh Mint Leaves):** 1/4 cup, finely chopped
* **Hara Dhania (Fresh Coriander Leaves):** 1/4 cup, finely chopped

## Preparation
1. **Drain the Lentils:** Completely drain the water from the soaked chana dal.
2. **Clean the Meat:** Ensure the minced mutton is completely dry. Excess moisture will prevent the kebabs from holding their shape.
3. **Herb Prep:** Keep the finely chopped pyaz, hari mirch, pudina, and hara dhania ready in a separate bowl for the final folding stage.

## Cooking Steps
1. **Boiling:** In a heavy-bottomed handi or pressure cooker, combine the minced mutton, soaked chana dal, adrak, lahsun, salt, and all the whole spices. Add just enough water to partially cover the mixture (around 1/2 to 3/4 cup). 
2. **Cooking Time:** Cook on medium heat until the meat is completely tender and the dal is soft enough to smash easily.
3. **Drying the Moisture:** Open the lid and check for any remaining liquid. If water remains, cook on high heat, stirring continuously, until the mixture is completely dry. Remove from heat and let it cool.
4. **Grinding:** Remove the larger whole spices (like the dalchini and badi elaichi hulls). Grind the meat and lentil mixture into a super-fine, smooth paste without adding any water. The texture must be fibrous and silky (*Reshedar*).
5. **Mixing Herbs:** Add the finely chopped pyaz, hari mirch, pudina, hara dhania, and the whisked egg into the kebab paste. Mix thoroughly using your hands.
6. **Shaping:** Divide the mixture into equal portions and shape them into smooth, round, flat patties with no cracks on the edges.
7. **Shallow Frying:** Heat asli ghee in a flat non-stick skillet. Shallow fry the patties on medium-low heat for 3-4 minutes on each side until they develop a crisp, deep golden-brown crust.

## When to Eat
* **Occasions:** Served as a premium starter during festive Shahi gatherings, Eid feasts, and formal daawats.
* **Meal Timing:** Best enjoyed as a premium evening snack with tea or as an opening course for lunch or dinner.

## With What to Eat
* **Accompaniments:** Pair it hot with ultra-thin *Rumali Roti* or layered *Khamiri Roti*. 
* **Condiments:** Must be served alongside spicy mint-coriander yogurt chutney and thinly sliced onion rings drizzled with fresh lemon juice.

## Chef's Secrets
* **The Moisture Trap:** The biggest failure point in Shami Kebab is runny dough. If your paste turns out loose, roast a tablespoon of gram flour (*besan*) and mix it in to absorb the excess moisture.
* **The Fibrous Texture:** Traditional khansamas never use an electric mixer-grinder to process the cooked mix; they use a stone mortar and pestle (*Sil-Batta*). This keeps the meat fibers intact, producing the iconic melt-in-the-mouth texture.
